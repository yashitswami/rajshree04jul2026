I. Executive summary

She isn't struggling to solve. She's choosing not to try.

The headline is a paradox: her accuracy on questions she attempts is very good, but 1 in 6 questions never gets an answer. The gap between "attempt" and "avoid" — not the gap between "right" and "wrong" — is where the score is being lost.

Read those two rates side by side. When Rajshree engages with a question, four out of five come out right — that is a strong signal of conceptual mastery. But her attempt rate on hard problems collapses: at the highest problem-complexity tier (PC=4) she skips 58% of questions outright. The score lost to skipping is larger than the score lost to being wrong. This report is a map of where and why that happens, and what to do about it.

The one-line diagnosis

Rajshree's ceiling isn't her capability. It's her willingness to engage with hard Maths and mechanics questions. Two subjects show this clearly: Chemistry (7.9% skip) and Mathematics (22.2% skip) have almost identical attempted accuracy — 80.8% and 80.6% — meaning the difference between them is entirely a decision to attempt, not an ability to solve.

V. The psychological pattern

The confidence gradient runs down the difficulty scale.

The single most important chart in this report is below. It shows the percentage of questions Rajshree skips as difficulty (problem complexity) increases — split by subject. Chemistry stays flat. Physics slopes up sharply. Mathematics is nearly a wall.

The critical observation: on the questions Rajshree attempts, her accuracy at PC=3 is nearly the same as her accuracy at PC=1 — 76–81% across all three subjects. That means her difficulty ceiling as a solver is far higher than her difficulty ceiling as an attempter. The bottleneck lives in the moment of deciding to try.

What this pattern typically means

1. Pattern recognition, not conceptual recall. She has trained herself to recognise problem types from PC=1 and 2. When a PC=3 problem arrives, it doesn't fit a template she has seen — so instead of building the solution, she treats "I don't recognise this" as "I can't solve this." The evidence: when she does attempt PC=3, she gets 74–81% right. She could solve them; she doesn't feel she could.

2. Time-anxiety avoidance. Notice the TC=3 columns in the chapter tables. In Maths especially, TC=3 accuracy is near-zero because those questions are skipped, not attempted-and-wrong. A student who has been coached to "protect time" often over-applies the rule — she is skipping to protect a clock that isn't running out.

3. Subject-linked self-image. The fact that Chemistry doesn't show this pattern is diagnostically important. She thinks of herself as "good at Chemistry" and therefore engages. She thinks of herself as "not the Maths one" and therefore withdraws. This is a self-concept problem more than a syllabus problem.

The independence question

Are the wrong answers concentrated in the same chapters as the skips, or are they independent? The tables in Section III answer this: they are largely independent. Permutations & Combinations, SHM, Centre of Mass and Newton's Laws are the "high-wrong" chapters. Quadratic Equations, Limits, Inverse Trig, Sequences & Series and Rotational Motion are the "high-skip" chapters. Different chapters need different interventions.

High-wrong chapters need concept revision — the underlying idea is not landed. High-skip chapters need attempt-training — the concept is likely there, but the engagement isn't.

VII. The improvement plan

Six changes, ordered by leverage.

These are ordered by how much score they should return per hour of effort — starting with the highest-leverage intervention. The first is a behavioural change, not a syllabus change, and it is expected to add the most marks.

i

The "attempt something" rule for Maths

For the next 30 days of practice, Rajshree writes at least one line of working for every Maths question — even the ones she would normally skip. The line can be "I would draw a diagram," "let x = the unknown," or "this uses the formula for …" She is not required to solve. She is required to begin.

Rationale: her attempted-accuracy in Maths is 80.6% — nearly identical to Chemistry. The gap is not knowledge; it is the moment of not-beginning. Forcing a first line short-circuits the skip decision.

ii

Rebuild mechanics from scratch — not chapter by chapter, but concept-first

SHM, Rotational Motion, Centre of Mass, Newton's Laws, Fluid Mechanics and Gravitation all cluster in the weak list. Instead of "revise SHM this week," spend two weeks re-deriving core mechanics concepts (frames of reference, conservation laws, moment of inertia, oscillation). Then re-test the chapters together in a single 60-question set.

Rationale: her PC=1 mechanics accuracy is good (formula plug-in). PC=2 collapses. This is the signature of formulaic learning without physical intuition.

iii

Re-test the "silent" weak chapters within 21 days

Four chapters have not had a real re-test since they went badly: Simple Harmonic Motion, Function, Sequences & Series, and Centre of Mass. Schedule a targeted 20-question test on each, mixing PC=1, 2 and 3. Whatever the outcome is, then design revision from the actual result — not from the assumption that "it was covered."

Rationale: without deliberate re-testing these appear resolved in the surface stats but are almost certainly still weak.

iv

Fix Permutations & Combinations by sub-topic

This is her single most error-prone Maths chapter (18 wrong out of 55 attempted). Break it into arrangement-with-repetition, circular permutations, combinations with restrictions, and distribution problems. Do 15 questions on each sub-topic and see which one drives the errors before general revision.

Rationale: unlike the skip-driven chapters, P&C failures are attempts. That means the concept-to-application step is broken somewhere specific.

v

Practise PC=3 in Chemistry for transfer

Counter-intuitive, but strategic. She already engages with hard Chemistry. Give her more PC=3 Chemistry — Ionic Equilibrium, Chemical Kinetics, Solutions numericals. The goal is not more Chemistry marks (she is already at 80% there). The goal is to strengthen the internal experience of "I attempted a hard question and got it right," which then transfers to Physics and Maths.

Rationale: the block is a self-image about engagement with hard questions. Repeated positive experiences of engagement rebuild it.

vi

Introduce a wrong-answer log — because the error rate isn't falling

The wrong-rate has been flat at ~17% for nine months. That means her current revision process is not converting mistakes into future correct answers. Start a physical notebook: every wrong answer gets one page — the question, her working, the correct method, and the specific mistake in words ("used the wrong formula," "sign error," "misread the question"). Review it weekly. Track whether the same mistake-type repeats.

Rationale: without an error-taxonomy she cannot see her own patterns. The flat wrong-rate is a signal that mistakes are not being retained as lessons.

A note on volume

Rajshree is doing enough practice — 3,400 attempts across 299 tests in nine months is high volume. Adding more tests will not help. The interventions above are all about quality of engagement per test, not the number of tests. If anything, she may benefit from slightly fewer but more deliberate test sessions, with more time spent on the wrong-answer log afterwards.
