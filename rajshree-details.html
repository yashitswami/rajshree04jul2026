V · The psychological pattern 04 / 09
The confidence chart
Skip rate climbs with problem complexity.
The wall is steepest in Maths.
Each line shows the percentage of questions Rajshree leaves blank at each problem-complexity tier. Chemistry stays flat. Physics slopes up. Mathematics is nearly a wall.
What the pattern means
The bottleneck lives in the moment of deciding to try.
Critical observation: on the questions Rajshree attempts, her accuracy at PC=3 is 76 – 81% across all three subjects. Nearly identical to PC=1.
Her difficulty ceiling as a solver is far higher than her difficulty ceiling as an attempter.
Three likely drivers
1. Pattern recognition, not concept recall. When a PC=3 problem doesn't match a template she has seen, she reads "unfamiliar" as "unsolvable."
2. Over-applied time-anxiety. A skip-to-save-time habit generalising to time she doesn't actually need.
3. Subject-linked self-image. "Good at Chemistry" → she tries. "Not the Maths one" → she withdraws.

VII · The improvement plan 09 / 09
Six changes, ordered by leverage
Behaviour first. Concept second. Retention last.
Ordered by expected score return per hour of effort. The first is a behavioural change, not a syllabus change — and it should add the most marks.
i
Behavioural · daily

Write one line for every Maths question.

For 30 days, one line of working on every Maths question — even the skip-tempters. "Let x = the unknown." "This uses the formula for …" She is not required to solve. She is required to begin.

Attempted acc. in Maths = 80.6%. The gap is not knowledge; it is the moment of not-beginning. Forcing a first line short-circuits the skip decision.

ii
Concept · two-week block

Rebuild mechanics concept-first.

Not chapter-by-chapter. Two weeks re-deriving core mechanics (frames of reference, conservation laws, moment of inertia, oscillation). Then re-test SHM, Centre of Mass, Rotational Motion, Newton's Laws, Fluid Mechanics, Gravitation together — one 60-question set.

PC=1 mechanics is fine (formula plug-in). PC=2 collapses. The signature of formulaic learning without physical intuition.

iii
Audit · within 21 days

Re-test the four "silent" chapters.

SHM, Function, Sequences & Series, Centre of Mass. Twenty questions each, mixing PC=1, 2, 3. Whatever the outcome, design revision from the actual result — not from the assumption that "it was covered."

Without deliberate re-testing these appear resolved in the surface stats but are almost certainly still weak.

iv
Targeted · one chapter

Break P&C by sub-topic.

Single most error-prone Maths chapter (18/55 wrong). Split into arrangement-with-repetition, circular permutations, combinations-with-restrictions, distribution. 15 questions on each, see which drives the errors before general revision.

Unlike skip-driven chapters, P&C failures are attempts. That means the concept-to-application step is broken somewhere specific.

v
Transfer · counter-intuitive

Practise PC=3 Chemistry to transfer confidence.

She already engages with hard Chemistry. Give her more PC=3 Chemistry — Ionic Equilibrium, Chemical Kinetics, Solutions numericals. Goal is not more Chemistry marks. Goal is the repeated internal experience of "I attempted a hard question and got it right."

The block is a self-image about engagement with hard questions. Repeated positive experiences of engagement rebuild it.

vi
Retention · weekly

Start a physical wrong-answer log.

Wrong-rate has been flat at ~17% for nine months. That means current revision is not converting mistakes into future correct answers. One page per wrong answer: the question, her working, the correct method, and the mistake in words ("sign error," "wrong formula"). Weekly review.

Without an error taxonomy she cannot see her own patterns. The flat wrong-rate is the signal.

A note on volume
Rajshree is doing enough practice — 3,400 attempts across 299 tests in nine months is high volume. Adding more tests will not help. These interventions are all about quality of engagement per test, not the number of tests. Fewer but more deliberate sessions, with more time spent on the wrong-answer log afterwards.
